Background

What is OSHC — and who needs it?

Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC) is a mandatory health insurance product for all international students studying in Australia on a Subclass 500 student visa. It is a legal requirement — you cannot obtain a student visa without it, and you must maintain it for the entire duration of your visa. OSHC covers you for hospital treatment, GP visits, emergency ambulance services, and some prescription medicines while you are in Australia.

OSHC is not the same as Medicare. International students cannot access Australia's Medicare system (with very limited exceptions for students from countries with reciprocal health agreements). OSHC fills this gap by providing equivalent health coverage for the duration of your studies.

There are four government-approved OSHC providers in Australia: Allianz Care, Bupa, Medibank, and NIB. All four meet the Australian Government's minimum standards for OSHC. The differences between them lie in price, GP network size, prescription medicine coverage, and digital services. Buying tips

5 things to know before you buy OSHC

01

Your cover must match your visa dates

OSHC must start on or before your course commencement date and end on or after your course end date. If your visa is extended, extend your OSHC immediately — a gap in cover is a visa condition breach.

02

You don't have to use your university's provider

Many universities suggest a default OSHC provider during enrolment. You are not obliged to use them. You can buy from any government-approved provider, often at the same or lower price.

03

Buy after you receive your CoE

Your Confirmation of Enrolment (CoE) contains the official course start and end dates. Buy OSHC after receiving your CoE so you enter the exact correct dates — and avoid paying for unnecessary extra months.

04

Family cover adds up — compare carefully

If your spouse and children are joining you in Australia, family cover is significantly more expensive than single cover. All four providers offer family policies — compare them carefully as price differences are larger for family cover.

05

You can get a partial refund if you leave early

If you complete your course early or your visa is refused, you may be eligible for a refund on the unused portion of your OSHC. Contact your provider directly — the process is straightforward for most providers.

06

Cheapest isn't always best value

The cheapest provider has fewer agreements with medical practices, meaning longer waits and more out-of-pocket expenses. Consider network size and claims reputation alongside price. Allianz has the widest GP and hospital network in Australia.

Which provider?

Which OSHC provider should you choose?

All four providers meet Australian Government minimum standards — so the decision comes down to what matters most to you: price, network coverage, GP rebate percentage, or digital experience. Here is a quick guide based on what different types of students typically prioritise.

If price is your priority → NIB or Allianz
NIB consistently offers competitive single-student pricing, particularly for shorter policies. Allianz is often competitive for family cover. Compare both using the live tool above with your exact dates.
If GP coverage matters → Bupa or NIB
Both Bupa and NIB pay 100% of the Medicare Benefit Schedule (MBS) for GP visits. Allianz and Medibank pay 85%, meaning you may have a small out-of-pocket gap at some GP clinics.
If network size matters → Allianz
Allianz has the widest hospital and GP network of any OSHC provider, and is the only provider offering gap-free services at member facilities — meaning zero out-of-pocket cost at participating providers.
If prescription medicines matter → Medibank
Medibank covers up to AUD 70 per prescription item — higher than the AUD 50 cap offered by the other three providers. If you take regular prescription medication, this difference is worth factoring in.

The best approach is to use the live comparator with your exact dates, then review the cheapest 1–2 options against the features table above. For most single students, the price difference between providers is AUD 50–150 per year — meaningful but not decisive. For families, the difference can be AUD 300–600 per year.
