📘 DILP Guide

Overview

The Australia Awards Scholarships are the Australian Government's flagship international scholarship program, managed by the Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade (DFAT). They are among the most generous scholarships available to citizens of developing countries — covering all major costs of studying in Australia, including full tuition, return airfare, and a fortnightly living allowance for the full duration of your degree.

Australia Awards have been running since 1955 and have supported over 100,000 alumni across the Indo-Pacific, Africa, and the Middle East. Scholars are selected not just on academic merit but on leadership potential and the strength of their plan to apply their Australian qualification to their home country's development challenges. This is what makes the program distinct from purely academic scholarships — and why the DILP (Development Impact and Linkages Plan) is the most critical document in your application.

What the scholarship covers

  • Full tuition fees at any participating Australian university or TAFE — for the complete duration of your program
  • Return economy class airfare (most direct route between your home country and Australia)
  • Contribution to Living Expenses (CLE) — currently AUD 29,710+ per year, paid fortnightly to your Australian bank account
  • Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC) for the award holder — you do not pay for health insurance separately
  • Establishment allowance — a one-off payment on arrival to cover initial setup costs (bedding, kitchenware, etc.)
  • Introductory Academic Program (IAP) — a pre-study orientation program in Australia, covering academic skills, cultural adjustment, and professional development
  • Pre-Course English (PCE) training — if your English score is below the required standard, DFAT funds English language training before your degree begins
  • Academic support and mentoring throughout your program, coordinated by the scholarship manager at your institution

Eligibility requirements

  • Citizen and resident of a participating country — approximately 50 countries across Asia, Pacific, Africa, and the Middle East
  • Not an Australian citizen, permanent resident, or applicant for Australian permanent residency
  • Not currently studying or residing in Australia at the time of application
  • IELTS Academic 6.5 overall with no band below 6.0 (or equivalent PTE/TOEFL) — valid as of 1 January of your proposed study commencement year
  • At least 2 years of relevant post-bachelor's work experience (for postgraduate applications)
  • Meet the academic entry requirements of your nominated Australian institution
  • Demonstrate leadership potential and a credible plan to use your qualification for development impact in your home country

Eligibility for specific study fields and levels varies by country. Check the DFAT country profile for your home country at dfat.gov.au for country-specific requirements and any additional criteria.

Priority sectors for 2027

Each participating country has DFAT-determined priority sectors — fields where Australia Awards investment is expected to deliver the most development impact. Applicants whose proposed study aligns directly with their country's priority areas are significantly more competitive at shortlisting.

Priority sectors vary by country, but commonly include across the Indo-Pacific and Africa:

  • Health systems and public health — epidemiology, health policy, nursing, medicine, and public health management
  • Infrastructure and engineering — civil, water, energy, and transport engineering; urban planning; water and sanitation (WASH)
  • Agriculture and food security — agronomy, agricultural economics, food systems, and climate-resilient farming
  • Education quality and access — education policy, curriculum development, STEM education, and teacher training
  • Governance and public policy — public administration, law, economics, financial management, and accountability
  • Climate change and environment — environmental science, disaster risk reduction, climate adaptation, and marine science
  • Gender equality — social policy, development studies, and public health with a gender focus

Download your country's DFAT country profile before applying. Priority areas can change between cycles. Study outside the listed areas is not automatically disqualified, but your DILP must make a compelling case for why your field contributes to national development goals.

The DILP — the most important document in your application

The Development Impact and Linkages Plan (DILP) is formally scored by DFAT assessors and is the single most consequential document in your application. It is not a personal statement or motivation letter — it is a structured development plan with two distinct parts:

  • Part 1 — Study and Development Linkages Plan: What you plan to study, how it connects to your home country's specific development challenges, and how you will apply your learning upon return.
  • Part 2 — Development Leadership Plan: A specific, credible plan for how you will contribute to measurable development outcomes in your home country after completing your scholarship. Assessors look for named organisations, specific roles, realistic timelines, and impact that can be measured.

Most Australia Awards applications succeed or fail on the DILP. A candidate with an average academic record and a specific, well-argued DILP will consistently outscore a candidate with excellent grades and a vague plan. The most common rejection reasons are: generic goals ("improve healthcare in my country"), no named organisation to return to, and impact that cannot be measured or attributed to the scholarship.

How to apply — step by step

Applications are submitted through the OASIS online portal at australiaawards.gov.au. The portal typically opens in February and closes at 14:00 AEST on 30 April each year. Late submissions are not accepted under any circumstances.

  1. January — Check your country profile and book IELTS: Download the current DFAT country profile for your home country. Confirm your proposed field is aligned with priority areas. Book your IELTS test — centres in Lahore, Dhaka, Lagos, and Abuja fill quickly in February and March. Aim for a January or February sitting to allow time for a resit.
  2. February — Register on OASIS and begin your DILP: Register your OASIS account as soon as the portal opens. Begin drafting your DILP immediately — allow 3–4 weeks for drafting and revision. This is not a document you should write in the final week.
  3. March — Gather documents and contact referees: Collect academic transcripts (with any required attestation or translation), employment verification letters, and police clearance. Contact your two referees and give them at least 4 weeks to complete their reports.
  4. April — Finalise and submit early: Review your complete application — particularly your DILP. Nominate up to three courses and three Australian institutions in order of preference. Submit at least one week before 30 April — the OASIS system experiences heavy traffic and occasional technical issues in the final days.
  5. June–August — Shortlisting and interviews: Shortlisted candidates are contacted for in-country interviews at the Australian High Commission or Embassy. Prepare to clearly explain your DILP, your return plan, and how your proposed qualification addresses your home country's development challenges.
  6. October–December — Outcomes: Final outcomes are announced between October and December. Successful scholars receive a formal offer and begin coordinating their visa, pre-departure briefings, and English training with the scholarship manager.

Application checklist

  • Valid passport — must cover the full study period
  • Proof of citizenship of a participating country
  • Academic transcripts for all qualifications — with attestation or translation where required by your country
  • IELTS Academic 6.5+ (no band below 6.0) — or equivalent PTE Academic / TOEFL iBT score
  • Statement of purpose / personal statement
  • Development Impact and Linkages Plan (DILP) — Parts 1 and 2
  • Curriculum vitae (CV) — max 4 pages, highlighting leadership, development-sector experience, and professional achievements
  • Two referee reports — completed using DFAT referee templates available on OASIS
  • Employment verification letter — confirming your role, responsibilities, and duration of employment (at least 2 years post-bachelor's)
  • Country-specific documents — check your DFAT country profile for any additional requirements (medical certificates, police clearance, etc.)

The two-year home return condition

Australia Awards Scholarships carry a legally binding condition: you must return to your home country and reside there for at least two continuous years after completing your scholarship, before applying for any other Australian visa. This condition is formally attached to your student visa and monitored by DFAT.

During the two-year period you cannot apply for any Australian visa, including: the Temporary Graduate visa (485), employer-sponsored visas (482, 186, 494), or skilled migration visas (189, 190, 491). Failure to comply results in a debt to the Commonwealth equal to the total scholarship cost — which for a two-year Master's program can exceed AUD 150,000.

The two-year period begins from your scholarship completion date, not your departure from Australia. This condition does not apply to university research scholarships (MGRS, USYDIS, Monash MGS+MITS) — only to Australia Awards government scholarships.
