What is IELTS and which version do you need?

IELTS (International English Language Testing System) is one of the world's most widely accepted English proficiency tests, co-owned by the British Council, IDP Education Australia, and Cambridge Assessment English. For international students applying to Australian universities or scholarships, it is the most commonly required test.

There are two versions:

🎓

IELTS Academic

Required for university admission, scholarship applications, and professional registration in Australia. If you are applying for Australia Awards, a research scholarship, or any coursework degree — this is the version you need.

💼

IELTS General Training

Used for work visas, migration, and some vocational training programs. Not accepted for university admission or most scholarship applications. Do not take this version if your goal is studying in Australia.

Important — always take IELTS Academic

If you are applying for any Australian university scholarship or student visa, you need IELTS Academic, not General Training. Submitting a General Training result will disqualify your application.

The 6.0 band rule — the most common trap

An overall score of 6.5 is not enough on its own. If any individual band (Listening, Reading, Writing, or Speaking) falls below 6.0, your application will be rejected — even with a 6.5 or 7.0 overall. The most commonly failed band for non-native speakers is Writing. Target at least 6.0 in every band, with 6.5 in Writing if possible.

IELTS score requirements for the Australian student visa

The Subclass 500 student visa has its own English requirements, which are separate from the scholarship requirements. In practice, your university's admission requirement (usually 6.5) is almost always higher than the visa minimum, so if you've met the university requirement, you've automatically met the visa requirement.

The IELTS test format

IELTS Academic tests four skills: Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking. The total test time is approximately 2 hours 45 minutes. Here is exactly what to expect in each section:

Listening — 30 minutes + 10 minutes transfer time

Four recorded monologues and conversations. 40 questions total. You hear each recording once. Questions include multiple choice, matching, plan/map/diagram labelling, form completion, and note completion. The accents are varied — Australian, British, American, and Canadian.

Band score tip: The listening section is where most test-takers score highest. Target 32+ correct answers (out of 40) for a band 7.0.

Reading — 60 minutes

Three long reading passages, typically from academic journals, newspapers, and books. 40 questions including True/False/Not Given, matching headings, sentence completion, and short answers. The texts are challenging — complex vocabulary and academic argumentation.

Band score tip: Time management is critical. Don't spend more than 20 minutes on each passage. Many test-takers run out of time on the third passage.

Writing — 60 minutes

Two tasks:

  • Task 1 (20 min, 150 words min): Describe a graph, chart, diagram or map using academic language. You are assessed on how accurately and completely you describe the data — not on your opinion.
  • Task 2 (40 min, 250 words min): Write an essay responding to a point of view, argument, or problem. Task 2 carries twice the weight of Task 1. A weak Task 2 significantly drags your overall Writing band.

Band score tip: Writing is the most commonly failed band for scholarship applicants. The most common mistakes are: not addressing the full question in Task 2, writing below the minimum word count, and poor paragraph structure. Practice Task 2 under timed conditions at least twice per week.

Speaking — 11–14 minutes

A face-to-face interview with a certified examiner (or a recorded interview in some computer-based formats). Three parts:

  • Part 1: Familiar topics — home, family, work, interests (4–5 minutes)
  • Part 2: Long turn — speak for 1–2 minutes on a given topic using notes (3–4 minutes)
  • Part 3: Discussion of abstract ideas related to Part 2 topic (4–5 minutes)

Band score tip: Speaking is assessed on Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range, and Pronunciation. You are NOT penalised for having an accent — only for being difficult to understand. Practice speaking continuously for 2 minutes without stopping.

Paper vs. Computer — which is better?

Computer-delivered IELTS: Results in 3–5 days. More test dates available (up to 7 days/week). Typing is faster for most people for Writing tasks. Same content, same marking, same scores. Paper-based IELTS: Results in 13 days. Available 2–4 times per month. Better if you are more comfortable handwriting. Both are equally accepted everywhere.

Book early — especially in Pakistan and Nigeria

IELTS test centre slots in Lahore, Karachi, Islamabad, Lagos, and Abuja fill up 6–10 weeks in advance during peak application season (February–April and September–November). Book your test date before you start preparing — it creates a deadline that improves your focus.

IELTS One Skill Retake — the game changer

Since 2023, IELTS has introduced the One Skill Retake (OSR) option. If you pass three bands but fail one (e.g., you get 7.0 overall but your Writing band is 5.5), you can retake just that one section within 60 days of your original test — without sitting the full exam again.

This is particularly useful for scholarship applicants who score 6.5+ overall but fall below 6.0 in one band. Check whether OSR is available at your test centre — it is now available in most major cities in Pakistan, India, Bangladesh, Nigeria and Australia.

Frequently asked questions

Australia Awards requires IELTS Academic 6.5 overall with no individual band below 6.0. The test result must be valid (within 2 years) as of 1 January of your study commencement year. PTE Academic 64+ is also accepted.

Most Australian universities require IELTS Academic 6.5 overall (no band below 6.0). Programs in law, medicine, nursing, and education typically require 7.0 overall. Always check the specific program's requirements on the university's official website.

The Subclass 500 student visa minimum is IELTS 5.5 overall, but your university's admission requirement (usually 6.5) is almost always higher and takes precedence in practice. If you've met the university requirement, you've met the visa requirement.

IELTS results are valid for 2 years from the test date. For Australia Awards, your result must be valid as of 1 January of the year you start your scholarship — meaning if you start in February 2027, your test must have been taken on or after 1 January 2025.

Yes — PTE Academic is accepted by most Australian universities and for the student visa. Australia Awards also accepts PTE Academic (minimum 64 overall). PTE has the advantage of faster results (24–48 hours) and is fully computer-based. TOEFL iBT is also accepted by most institutions but not all scholarships — check the specific requirements.

There is no limit to the number of times you can take IELTS. You can retake it as many times as needed. Many successful scholarship applicants took IELTS 2–3 times before achieving their target score. The IELTS One Skill Retake option also allows you to retake just one failed band within 60 days of your original test.

It is ideal for test-takers who pass three bands but fall below 6.0 in one. Check whether OSR is available at your test centre before booking your original test.
