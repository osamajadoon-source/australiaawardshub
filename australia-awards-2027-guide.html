Australia Awards Scholarships 2027: The Complete Guide

Everything international students need to know to apply for Australia's most prestigious fully-funded scholarship — eligibility, benefits, the OASIS portal, deadlines, documents, and tips to make your application stand out.

The Australia Awards Scholarships are widely considered the gold standard of scholarships for international students from developing countries. Funded by the Australian Government and administered by the Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade (DFAT), they offer something rare in the scholarship world: a genuinely fully-funded package — tuition, airfare, living expenses, and health cover, all included.

But the program is also competitive, paperwork-heavy, and tightly regulated. This guide walks you through every step of the 2027 application cycle so you can submit a strong, complete application — and avoid the traps that catch many first-time applicants.

Heads up: 2027 application status

Applications for the 2027 intake opened on 1 February 2026 and close on 30 April 2026 at 14:00 AEST. If you're reading this after the deadline, the next intake (2028) typically opens in February 2027. The process described below repeats each year.

1. What are Australia Awards Scholarships?

Australia Awards Scholarships are long-term scholarships offered by the Australian Government to citizens of developing countries — primarily in the Indo-Pacific region, but also Africa and the Middle East. They're a core part of Australia's international development policy: the goal is to help build the skills and networks of future leaders in partner nations.

The scheme is run by DFAT and delivered in partnership with participating Australian universities and TAFE (vocational training) institutions. In a typical year, around 1,000 scholarships are awarded across more than 50 partner countries.

The scholarships fund full-time undergraduate or postgraduate study, depending on the partner country's agreement with Australia. In many countries (such as those in South Asia and Africa), the program is restricted to Master's degrees and PhDs.

Who runs the program in your country?

While DFAT sets the global policy, applications are managed in-country. South Asia and Mongolia are run through Australia Awards South Asia & Mongolia (with country offices in Nepal, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, etc.), Africa is run through Australia Awards Africa, and the Pacific has its own regional office. Each country has its own Country Profile document outlining specific eligibility rules, priority study fields, and document requirements — always read yours.

2. Who can apply: eligibility & countries

General eligibility (applies to everyone)

  • You must be a citizen of a participating country (full list below).
  • You must be at least 18 years old on 1 February of the year your scholarship would commence.
  • You must not be an Australian citizen, hold permanent residency, or be applying for a visa to live in Australia permanently.
  • You must not be married to, engaged to, or in a de facto relationship with someone who holds (or is eligible for) Australian or New Zealand citizenship/permanent residency.
  • You must satisfy DFAT's standard health and character requirements.
  • You must meet all the academic and English-language entry requirements of the Australian institution you want to study at.
  • You must not currently be undertaking a similar or higher-level qualification.

Country-specific extras

Most countries layer additional rules on top. For example:

  • Africa: Most African applicants must be a minimum of 25 years old and have at least 5 years of relevant post-graduate work experience.
  • Pacific countries (e.g. Solomon Islands): Postgraduate applicants must have 3 years of relevant work experience.
  • South Asia (Pakistan, Nepal, Sri Lanka, etc.): Tend to focus on Master's-level study and require relevant work experience tied to your proposed field.

Always read your specific Country Profile — it's the single most important document for your application.

3. What the scholarship covers

Australia Awards is one of the few global scholarships that's truly fully funded. The standard benefits package includes:

  • Full tuition fees for the duration of your degree.
  • Return economy airfare from your home country to Australia and back, via the most direct route.
  • Establishment allowance — a one-off payment to help with moving costs (accommodation deposit, textbooks, study materials).
  • Contribution to Living Expenses (CLE) — a fortnightly stipend for basic living expenses, paid at a rate set by DFAT.
  • Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC) for the full duration of your scholarship — covers most medical costs.
  • Introductory Academic Program (IAP) — a compulsory pre-study orientation covering academic skills and life in Australia.
  • Supplementary academic support if you need extra help to succeed.
  • Fieldwork allowance for research students whose courses include compulsory fieldwork.
  • Reasonable accommodations for scholars with disabilities.

What's not covered: family travel and living costs (you can bring your family on a student visa, but you'll need to support them yourself), and any expenses outside the standard package.

4. English language requirements

You must submit a valid English test result with your application. The minimum scores (which must be valid as of 1 January of your scholarship commencement year) are:

Lower scores for some applicants

Women, people with disability, members of marginalised groups, and (in some countries) public servants can apply with reduced scores: IELTS 6.0 with no band below 5.5 (or equivalent TOEFL/PTE). However, if selected, you'll still need to meet your university's standard score before commencing study.

Important: many Australian universities have their own English requirements that exceed the DFAT minimum. If your chosen course requires IELTS 7.0, that's the score you actually need.

5. Application timeline for 2027

The 2027 cycle follows the standard Australia Awards calendar:

  • 1 February 2026: OASIS portal opens; country offices announce information sessions.
  • February – March 2026: Information sessions held (often online via Microsoft Teams). Highly recommended.
  • 30 April 2026, 14:00 AEST: Hard deadline. Late applications are not accepted.
  • May – August 2026: Document review, shortlisting, interviews (in some countries).
  • September – November 2026: Final selections announced, contracts signed, university placements confirmed.
  • December 2026 – January 2027: Pre-departure briefings and IAP begin.
  • February 2027 onwards: Scholars commence study in Australia.

6. The 6-step application process

Step 1 — Confirm your eligibility

Before you spend hours on an application, double-check that you meet both the general eligibility rules and your country's specific extras. Most rejections happen here, not at selection — applicants assume they're eligible and only realise too late that they're missing a work experience requirement or English score.

Step 2 — Align your goals with your country's development priorities

Australia Awards is a development program, not a general academic scholarship. The selection panel wants to know: how will your degree help your home country? Read your Country Profile carefully. Each profile lists priority fields of study (e.g. health, agriculture, public policy, climate adaptation) — applications that align with these are weighted more favourably.

Step 3 — Research Australian universities and courses

You'll need to nominate up to two course preferences in your application, and you must enter the correct CRICOS course codes. CRICOS is the official register of courses available to international students — search it at cricos.education.gov.au. Confirm directly with the university that the course is offered to international students for your intake year.

Step 4 — Prepare your supporting documents

Gather everything before you start the application form (full list in section 7). Scan academic transcripts, get your English test booked early, line up referees, and prepare your CV.

Step 5 — Submit your application via OASIS

Applications are submitted online through the Online Australia Scholarships Information System (OASIS), at oasis.dfat.gov.au. You'll register, get a username, and can save drafts before submitting. Two countries — Indonesia and the Philippines — use country-specific portals instead; check your Country Profile.

Don't wait until the last day

OASIS gets overloaded in the final 48 hours before the deadline. Submission failures, slow uploads, and lost progress are common. Aim to submit at least a week before the closing date.

Step 6 — Submit before the closing date

Once you submit, you can't edit. Late applications, incomplete applications, or applications missing required documents are rejected automatically. The deadline is firm: 30 April 2026, 14:00 AEST.

7. Documents you'll need

Exact requirements vary by country, but most applicants need:

  • Passport bio page (must be valid).
  • Birth certificate or national ID.
  • Academic transcripts for all degrees (with English translations if originals aren't in English).
  • Degree certificates.
  • English language test result (IELTS / TOEFL / PTE).
  • Up-to-date CV using the country-specific template if provided.
  • Two referee reports (one academic, one professional, in most countries) using the official referee template.
  • Statement of Service from your current/past employers (Pacific and African countries especially).
  • Proof of citizenship in the partner country.
  • Country-specific extras — e.g. Africa requires evidence of work experience and a Letter of No Objection from your employer.

8. Selection criteria — how to stand out

The selection panel scores applications on three broad areas:

  1. Academic competence — your grades, English score, and the rigour of your previous studies.
  2. Professional and personal qualities — leadership potential, communication skills, and ability to thrive in an Australian academic environment.
  3. Development impact — how clearly and credibly you can connect your proposed study to development outcomes in your home country.

What makes a strong application

  • Specificity beats generality. "I want to improve health in my country" is weak. "I want to improve maternal mortality outcomes in rural Sindh through district health system reform — building on my four years as a Lady Health Worker programme coordinator" is strong.
  • Show, don't tell. Concrete examples of leadership, impact, and initiative outperform abstract claims.
  • Connect every section to development. Your fields of study, your career history, and your future plans should form a coherent story.
  • Pick the right course. A Master's that closely matches a priority field for your country is much stronger than an "interesting" course in a non-priority area.
  • Get good referees. A strong, specific reference from someone who can speak to your work outperforms a generic glowing letter from a senior figure who barely knows you.
AI use is restricted

DFAT explicitly limits AI use to "basic tasks like spelling, grammar and organising ideas." AI must not be used to write, improve, or paraphrase your application. Misuse can lead to disqualification. Write in your own voice.

9. The 2-year return rule, explained

Every Australia Awards scholar signs a contract with the Commonwealth of Australia agreeing to return to their home country for a minimum of 2 years after completing their study. This is non-negotiable and central to the development purpose of the scholarship.

If you fail to comply (for instance, by staying in Australia on a different visa), you become liable for the full accrued cost of your scholarship — typically AUD $200,000–$400,000 depending on the program. This is treated as a debt to the Australian Government.

This rule isn't a deal-breaker — most scholars happily return — but you should think hard about it before applying. If your career plan only makes sense in Australia, this isn't the right scholarship for you, and there are better-aligned alternatives like the Sydney USYDIS or Melbourne Graduate Research Scholarship.

10. Avoiding scams

Australia Awards is the most prestigious scholarship in many partner countries — which means it attracts scammers. Two key things to know:

  • The Australia Awards program never asks applicants for money or bank details. Anyone requesting payment for "processing", "interview slots", or "guaranteed selection" is running a scam. Report them.
  • The only legitimate application portal is OASIS. Third-party "agents" charging fees to "submit your application" are unnecessary at best and fraudulent at worst.

For trusted information, only use:

  • The official DFAT Australia Awards page
  • Your country's official Australia Awards website (e.g. australiaawardspakistan.org)
  • Study Australia for general info

11. Frequently asked questions

Can I apply if I'm already in Australia?

No. You must be residing in your country of citizenship at the time of application, and apply from there.

Can I apply for multiple intakes?

Yes — if you're unsuccessful, you can re-apply in subsequent years. Many successful scholars apply 2 or 3 times before being selected. Each year's application is assessed on its own merit.

Do I need a university offer before applying?

No. You apply for the scholarship first, then DFAT/Australia Awards facilitates your placement at one of your nominated universities. However, you should research courses thoroughly so you can list realistic, well-matched preferences.

Can I bring my family?

You can bring dependants on a student visa, but Australia Awards does not provide funding for them. You're responsible for their travel, living costs, school fees, and health insurance.

What's the success rate?

It varies by country, but typically 5–15% of applicants are successful — meaning competition is intense. Strong academic records, clear development alignment, and well-prepared applications give you the best shot.

Is there an interview?

Yes, in most countries. Shortlisted applicants are invited to an interview (often a panel format), usually held a few months after the application deadline. Interviews are competency-based and focus on your motivation, development plans, and how your study will benefit your country.

What happens if my IELTS expires before I commence?

IELTS is valid for 2 years from the test date. The DFAT rule is that your test must be valid as of 1 January of your commencement year. If yours expires before then, you'll need to re-test before being placed at your university.

Final word

An Australia Awards Scholarship is genuinely life-changing for the right candidate — fully-funded education at a world-class university, plus a global alumni network you'll keep for life. But it's also a competitive, paperwork-heavy process that rewards careful preparation. Start early, read your Country Profile in detail, and treat the application as a serious project, not a quick form to fill in.

If the 2027 deadline has passed by the time you read this, the 2028 cycle will open in February 2027. Bookmark this guide — the process repeats almost identically each year.

Good luck.
