CRICOS Australia 2026 — The Complete Guide for International Students

Before you apply to study in Australia, you need to understand CRICOS. Every university, college, and course you choose must be registered on this government list — or your student visa application will be rejected. This guide explains everything.

What is CRICOS?

CRICOS stands for the Commonwealth Register of Institutions and Courses for Overseas Students. It is the official Australian Government register — maintained by the Department of Education — that lists every education provider and every course that is legally approved to enrol international students on a student visa.

CRICOS was established under the Education Services for Overseas Students (ESOS) Act 2000, which is the legal framework that governs how Australian institutions treat international students. The ESOS Act protects international students' rights, ensures institutions meet quality standards, and creates a system of accountability for every provider that accepts fee-paying overseas students.

In simple terms: if a university, college, TAFE, language school, or vocational training provider wants to accept international students on a student visa, it must be registered on CRICOS. There are no exceptions.

The one rule you must remember

If a course or institution is not listed on CRICOS, you cannot apply for an Australian student visa using that enrolment. Always check CRICOS before paying any tuition deposit or accepting any offer letter.

Why CRICOS matters for your student visa

When you apply for a Subclass 500 student visa, the Department of Home Affairs requires a Confirmation of Enrolment (CoE). Your CoE is issued by your institution only after you have paid your fees and enrolled in a CRICOS-registered course. The CoE contains your CRICOS course code — and without a valid CoE from a CRICOS-registered institution, your visa application cannot proceed.

CRICOS registration also protects you as a student in three important ways:

  • Quality assurance — institutions must meet strict standards for teaching quality, student support, and infrastructure to maintain their CRICOS registration
  • Financial protection — under the ESOS Act, registered providers must maintain a Tuition Protection Service (TPS) arrangement, meaning your tuition fees are protected if the institution closes or cancels your course
  • Transparency — every CRICOS-registered course must publish its tuition fee, duration, course requirements, and entry criteria in the register

What is a CRICOS code?

Every institution and every individual course on the CRICOS register has its own unique code:

CRICOS Provider Code

Each registered institution has a CRICOS provider code — a 5-digit number followed by a letter (for example, 00116K for the University of Melbourne, 00026A for the Australian National University). This code identifies the institution on all official documents, visa applications, and the government register.

CRICOS Course Code

Each specific course at each institution also has its own CRICOS course code — a 6-digit number (for example, 093913C for a specific Master of Engineering program). This code ties a particular degree program to a particular campus of a particular university and confirms it is approved for international student enrolment.

Important — the same degree at different campuses has different codes

A Master of Business Administration at Monash University's Clayton campus has a different CRICOS code than the same degree at Monash's Melbourne CBD campus. Always check the code for the specific campus where you will study, not just the course name.

Types of providers on CRICOS

CRICOS covers every level and type of education provider in Australia. Understanding the types helps you choose the right institution for your goals:

Higher Education Providers (Universities)

Australia's universities are the most widely sought after by international students and offer the broadest range of CRICOS-registered programs. The Group of Eight (Go8) universities — Melbourne, Sydney, ANU, UNSW, Monash, UQ, UWA, and Adelaide — are the most research-intensive and carry the highest global rankings. All Australian public universities are CRICOS-registered.

Vocational Education and Training (VET) / TAFEs

Technical and Further Education (TAFE) institutes and registered training organisations (RTOs) offer certificate, diploma, and advanced diploma programs in trades, hospitality, business, IT, and healthcare. Many TAFE qualifications are pathway programs that allow students to progress into university degrees. Not all RTOs are CRICOS-registered — always verify before applying.

English Language Schools (ELICOS)

English Language Intensive Courses for Overseas Students (ELICOS) are short-term English language programs. These must be separately registered under ELICOS Standards in addition to CRICOS. Many international students undertake an ELICOS course before starting their university or TAFE program to improve their English language skills.

Foundation Program Providers

Foundation programs (sometimes called pathway programs or bridging courses) are designed for students who do not yet meet the direct entry requirements of Australian universities. They must meet separate CRICOS Foundation Program Standards. Completing a CRICOS-registered foundation program at a university can give you direct entry into the first year of a bachelor's degree.

Schools

Primary and secondary schools that enrol international students must also be CRICOS-registered. Students who study at a CRICOS-registered school are eligible for a student visa and may be accompanied by a parent or guardian who can apply for a guardian visa.

How to search the CRICOS register — step by step

1

Go to the official CRICOS website

Visit cricos.education.gov.au — this is the only official government source. It is free to search and requires no login.

2

Search for an institution

Click "Search for an Institution." Enter the university or college name. You will see the provider's CRICOS code, contact details, registered courses, and current registration status. Check that the registration status says "Registered" — not "Cancelled" or "Suspended."

3

Search for a specific course

Click "Search for a Course." You can filter by course name, field of education, state, and course level. Each result shows the CRICOS course code, tuition fee, duration, course requirements, and which campus offers it.

4

Verify the course code matches your offer letter

Your official offer letter from the university must include a CRICOS course code. Cross-check this code against the CRICOS register to confirm it is correct, active, and matches the campus and course you intend to study.

5

Keep your course code for your visa application

Your CRICOS course code will appear on your Confirmation of Enrolment (CoE) and is required for your Subclass 500 student visa application. Store it carefully — you will need it multiple times during the application process.

What CRICOS registration means for your rights

Choosing a CRICOS-registered institution gives you significant legal protections that non-registered providers cannot offer:

  • Tuition Protection Service (TPS) — if your institution closes or your course is cancelled, TPS ensures you are placed in an alternative course or receive a refund of your remaining prepaid tuition fees
  • Right to a written agreement — before you pay fees, your institution must provide you with a written agreement outlining fees, refund policy, and course details
  • Complaints and appeals process — all CRICOS providers must have a formal complaints and appeals process. If you have a dispute with your institution, you have a legal right to use it
  • Protection from misleading information — providers must not give you false or misleading information about courses, fees, outcomes, or employment prospects
  • Right to change providers — after six months of your principal course, you have the right to transfer to another CRICOS-registered institution without requiring your current institution's permission

What happens if you study at a non-CRICOS provider?

Studying at a provider that is not CRICOS-registered while on a student visa is a breach of your visa conditions and can result in visa cancellation. You also have no legal protections for your tuition fees, no Tuition Protection Service coverage, and no right to a student visa renewal. Never accept an enrolment offer without first verifying CRICOS registration.

CRICOS and the GTE (Genuine Temporary Entrant) requirement

When you apply for a student visa, the Department of Home Affairs assesses whether you are a genuine temporary entrant — meaning you intend to study in Australia temporarily and return home afterwards. The course you choose directly affects this assessment.

Selecting a CRICOS-registered course that is relevant to your career goals and aligned with your home country's needs strengthens your GTE. Assessors look for a clear connection between your study plans and your professional background. A civil engineer from Pakistan applying for a Master of Environmental Engineering has a strong and coherent narrative. A civil engineer applying for a Bachelor of Culinary Arts raises questions.

Tip — choose a CRICOS course that aligns with your scholarship priorities

If you are applying for Australia Awards, your CRICOS course must align with your country's development priority sectors (water, health, education, infrastructure, governance). Choosing a course that fits both your career goals and these priority areas strengthens both your visa GTE and your scholarship application simultaneously.

CRICOS registration and university accreditation are related but different. Accreditation (through bodies like TEQSA — the Tertiary Education Quality and Standards Agency) confirms that an institution meets quality standards for Australian higher education. A provider generally needs both: accreditation to operate and CRICOS registration to enrol international students.
