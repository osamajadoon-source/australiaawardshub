Fully Funded Scholarships in Australia for International Students (2026/2027 Guide)

📅 Updated April 2026 · ⏱ 12 min read · 🎓 For prospective students

Australia hosts more than 700,000 international students every year, and a significant share of them study on full scholarships. If you have strong academics, a clear study goal, and a willingness to do the paperwork properly, a fully funded place is genuinely within reach. This guide walks you through every credible pathway available in 2026 and 2027.

"Fully funded" can mean different things depending on the scholarship. The most generous awards in Australia cover all of the following: tuition fees, return airfare, monthly living stipend, single Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC), and a one-off establishment allowance. Less generous "fully funded" awards may cover just tuition and a stipend, leaving airfare and visa costs to you. Throughout this guide, we'll be specific about exactly what each scholarship covers so you can compare like-for-like.

Why Australia is one of the strongest scholarship destinations

Three things make Australia particularly attractive for funded study compared to the US, UK, or Canada:

  1. The Australian Government funds scholarships at scale. Through the Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade (DFAT) and the Research Training Program (RTP), the federal government directly funds thousands of international scholarships every year — not just for elite candidates, but for talented students from a long list of partner countries.
  2. Universities compete for international students. The Group of Eight (Sydney, Melbourne, ANU, UQ, UNSW, Monash, UWA, Adelaide) and major institutions like UTS and RMIT all run their own merit and equity scholarships to attract diverse cohorts.
  3. Post-study work rights are generous. Most graduates from Australian universities qualify for a Temporary Graduate visa (subclass 485), giving you 2–6 years of post-study work — which means scholarships often pay for themselves several times over.

The two main types of fully funded scholarships

1. Government-funded scholarships

These are the gold standard. They cover everything, run on annual cycles, and are highly competitive. The flagship is the Australia Awards Scholarships, funded by DFAT for citizens of around 50 partner countries across Asia, the Pacific, Africa, and the Middle East. Applications for the 2027 intake closed on 30 April 2026, and the next round will open in February 2027 for the 2028 intake.

The other major government program is the Research Training Program (RTP), which funds Master's by Research and PhD students at every Australian university. Unlike Australia Awards, RTP awards are administered by individual universities and have rolling or termly deadlines.

2. University-funded scholarships

Every Australian university runs its own scholarship suite. These typically fall into three buckets:

  • Fully funded research scholarships for PhD and Master's by Research students — usually a combination of a fee waiver (e.g. RTP International Fee Offset) and a stipend (e.g. Monash Graduate Scholarship, Sydney USYDIS, Melbourne Graduate Research Scholarship).
  • Partial coursework scholarships for taught Master's and undergraduate students — typically 20% to 50% tuition reduction, automatically considered with admission (e.g. UQ International Excellence, Monash Merit, RMIT Future Leaders, ANU Chancellor's).
  • Region- or country-specific scholarships — targeted at students from particular regions (e.g. the RMIT Future Leaders Scholarship for citizens of South Asia, or UTS scholarships for Southeast Asia).

Quick reality check

The phrase "fully funded coursework Master's scholarship for international students" is rare in Australia. Most fully funded packages are tied to research degrees (PhD, Master's by Research). If you want a coursework Master's funded, your best bets are Australia Awards (if your country is eligible) or partial fee-reduction scholarships stacked with personal funding.

Core eligibility requirements

While each scholarship has its own rules, every fully funded Australian scholarship will check the following before considering your application:

Academic merit

For research scholarships, you'll typically need a "First Class Honours" or equivalent — generally a GPA of around 3.5/4.0 or 75–80% in your most recent degree. For coursework scholarships, the bar is usually a strong upper-second-class degree (around 65–70%).

English language proficiency

For Australia Awards, the minimum is IELTS Academic 6.5 overall (no band below 6.0), or TOEFL iBT 84+, or PTE Academic 58+. Individual universities often require slightly higher scores (e.g. IELTS 7.0 for some Master's programs in education, law, or medicine). Test results must usually be valid as of the start of your study (e.g. 1 January 2027 for 2027 intake).

Country eligibility

Australia Awards is restricted to citizens of around 50 specific countries. Most university scholarships are open to all nationalities, but some have regional categories (e.g. ANU Chancellor's has nine separate categories including South Asia and Middle East/Africa).

Work experience (sometimes)

Australia Awards postgraduate applications generally expect at least two years of relevant professional experience post-bachelor's. University research scholarships are more flexible — research output (publications, projects) often counts more than work experience.

Application timeline (when to start)

The biggest mistake international students make is starting the process too late. Here's a realistic timeline for a 2027 intake:

  • 15–18 months before: Take or retake your IELTS/TOEFL/PTE. Identify 3–5 target programs/universities. Begin drafting your personal statement.
  • 12 months before: Approach two academic referees. Request transcripts and have them officially translated if needed. For research degrees, identify potential supervisors and email them with a research proposal.
  • 9–10 months before: Submit university admission applications. For Australia Awards specifically, applications open 1 February each year and close 30 April for the following year's intake.
  • 6 months before: Submit scholarship applications (if separate from admission). Follow up with referees.
  • 3 months before: Receive offers. Accept your scholarship and start the visa (subclass 500) process.

How to write a winning scholarship application

Selection panels read thousands of applications every year. Here are the patterns that consistently separate successful applicants from rejected ones:

Be specific about impact

Generic statements like "I want to contribute to my country's development" are forgettable. Specific statements — naming a problem, a sector, a region, and the role you want to play — get remembered. Compare:

"I want to help my country develop its infrastructure."

versus:

"After completing my Master's in Water Resources Engineering, I plan to return to KP Province in Pakistan and join the Water and Sanitation Services Peshawar to design climate-resilient drinking water systems for the 200+ rural villages currently outside the formal supply network."

The second answer signals research, planning, and personal commitment in one paragraph.

Show fit with the program

Selection panels want to know that you've actually researched the program you're applying to. Cite specific subjects, supervisors (for research degrees), or research centres at your target university. A line like "Professor X's recent paper on Y is directly relevant to my proposed research because Z" is gold.

Quantify everything you've done

Numbers anchor claims. "Led a team" is weaker than "Led a team of 8 across 3 sites for 14 months, delivering AUD 240k of work below budget." Pull numbers from your CV, projects, and academic transcripts wherever you can.

Get someone to read your draft

Ask someone who has won a scholarship — or a senior colleague who's been on a hiring panel — to read your application before you submit. They'll spot vague phrasing and weak structure that you can no longer see after staring at it for hours.

One AI warning

The Australian Government has explicitly stated that Australia Awards applicants may only use AI for spelling, grammar, and organising ideas. AI must not be used to write, improve, or paraphrase any part of your application. Misuse may result in disqualification. Universities are increasingly running similar checks. Write your own application — your authentic voice is what scholarship panels are trying to assess.

Common mistakes that get applications rejected

  • Missing the deadline by even a few hours. The Australia Awards portal closes at 14:00 AEST on 30 April. There are no extensions, no exceptions, and no recovery from a late submission.
  • Submitting an English test report with the wrong validity date. Most scholarships require results valid as of 1 January of the year you start. Check this carefully — IELTS results are valid for two years, but the validity must extend through your scholarship start date.
  • Generic personal statements reused across applications. Panels can spot a recycled statement instantly. Each application needs to be tailored to the specific program and country.
  • Applying for a course not offered to international students. Use the official CRICOS database to confirm a course is available to international students before listing it on your application.
  • Forgetting referee follow-up. Your referees need to submit their reports by the same deadline as you. Send them reminders 2 weeks and 3 days before the deadline.

What if you don't qualify for a fully funded scholarship?

Most international students don't get a 100% funded scholarship — and that's fine. There are several other paths to making study in Australia financially viable:

  • Stack a partial scholarship with savings or a loan. A 50% fee reduction on a Master's degree saves you AUD 25,000–35,000 over the program. Combined with on-campus part-time work (international students can work 48 hours per fortnight during term), this can be a sustainable model.
  • Apply for multiple partial scholarships at the same institution. Many universities allow you to combine, for example, a 20% merit scholarship with a faculty-specific equity bursary. Always read the terms carefully.
  • Consider scholarships from your home country. Many governments and private foundations fund international study. Australia Awards is just one piece of a much larger funding ecosystem.
  • Choose a less expensive university or city. Universities in Adelaide, Perth, and regional NSW are often AUD 10,000–15,000 per year cheaper than the Group of Eight in Sydney or Melbourne.

Frequently asked questions

Can I apply for multiple Australian scholarships at once?

Yes — and you should. Most university coursework scholarships are automatically considered when you apply for admission, which means you can effectively be in the running for several without doing extra work. Australia Awards is a separate application and can be combined with university applications.

Do I need to be admitted to a university before applying for a scholarship?

It depends. For Australia Awards, you apply for the scholarship first and the program selection happens during the process. For most university research scholarships (USYDIS, Melbourne Graduate Research, Monash MGS), you need an admission offer — or to be applying for one — at the time of scholarship application. For coursework merit scholarships (UQ Excellence, Monash Merit, RMIT Future Leaders), you're usually auto-considered once admitted.

How competitive are these scholarships?

Australia Awards has historically had an acceptance rate of around 5–10%, depending on the country. University research scholarships vary by faculty but are typically in the 10–20% range. Partial coursework scholarships are less competitive — often 30–50% of high-achieving offer-holders receive some form of fee reduction.

Will accepting a scholarship affect my ability to stay in Australia after graduation?

Australia Awards specifically requires you to leave Australia for at least two years after completing your scholarship. This is a non-negotiable condition. Most other scholarships (university and RTP) do not have this restriction — graduates are free to apply for the Temporary Graduate visa and stay on.

Can I work while on a fully funded scholarship?

Yes. International students on a subclass 500 visa can work up to 48 hours per fortnight during term, and unlimited hours during scheduled breaks. Many scholarship-holders work part-time to supplement their stipend, especially in cities like Sydney and Melbourne where the cost of living exceeds the stipend.

Next steps

The single most useful thing you can do today is:

  1. Identify three target scholarships from our scholarship hub.
  2. Note the deadline for each one and put it in your calendar.
  3. Book your IELTS, TOEFL, or PTE test for the next available date — even if you think your English is fine, having a current test score is a hard requirement.
  4. Start drafting your personal statement. Aim for 600–800 words. Have it ready in editable form so you can adapt it to each scholarship.

Australian scholarships reward students who plan early, write specifically, and follow instructions exactly. None of those things require a perfect academic record — they require deliberate effort over 12 to 18 months. If you start now, you'll be ahead of the majority of applicants.
