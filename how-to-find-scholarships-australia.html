How to Find Scholarships in Australia as an International Student: A Complete Guide

Australia offers some of the world's most generous scholarships to international students — fully funded government awards, research packages worth AUD 42,000+ per year, and automatic merit discounts at leading universities. This guide shows you exactly where to look and how to find the right one for your background.

There is no single place to find every Australian scholarship. Government scholarships, university research packages, and institutional merit awards all operate independently, have different deadlines, and target different applicants. The students who find the best funding are those who understand the landscape — not those who spend hours on the wrong databases.

The three types of scholarships available in Australia

Before you start searching, it helps to understand that Australian scholarships fall into three fundamentally different categories. Each works differently, funds different things, and suits a different type of applicant. Confusing them is the most common reason students waste time on applications they were never going to win.

1. Government scholarships

These are funded by the Australian Government (DFAT) and target citizens of developing countries. The flagship program is Australia Awards — one of the world's most generous scholarships, covering full tuition, return airfare, and a living allowance of AUD 29,710 per year. Also in this category: the Research Training Program (RTP) for research students, and the Destination Australia program for students willing to study at regional universities.

Government scholarships are highly competitive, take months to apply for, and carry conditions like the Australia Awards' two-year home return rule. They are most suitable for applicants with strong professional backgrounds and a clear development or research purpose.

2. University research scholarships

These are funded directly by individual Australian universities for postgraduate research students. They are the most financially generous category — the best packages cover full international tuition plus a living stipend of AUD 37,000–42,754 per year. Key examples:

Research scholarships require a confirmed or responsive supervisor at your target university before applying — this is the single most important step for this category. Without a supervisor, your application is unlikely to succeed regardless of your academic record.

3. University merit scholarships (coursework)

These are partial tuition reductions awarded to high-achieving international students applying for undergraduate or postgraduate coursework degrees. They are less competitive than the first two categories and are often assessed automatically with your admission application — meaning no extra form to fill in. Examples include the Monash International Merit Scholarship (AUD 10,000/year), the UQ International Excellence Scholarship (25% tuition reduction), and the ANU Chancellor's International Scholarship (25–50% reduction).

Where to actually search — the right sources

Most scholarship search engines return outdated or incomplete results for Australia. Here is where to search, in order of reliability:

For government scholarships

  • dfat.gov.au/australia-awards — the official Australia Awards website. Check your country's profile page for current priority areas, eligibility requirements, and the annual opening date for applications.
  • australiaawards.gov.au — the OASIS application portal for Australia Awards. Register early each year — the portal opens in February.

For university research scholarships

  • Each university's graduate research page — for Melbourne, go to scholarships.unimelb.edu.au; for Sydney, sydney.edu.au/scholarships; for Monash, monash.edu/graduate-research. There is no central database that reliably lists all research scholarships.
  • Find a supervisor first — browse research.unimelb.edu.au, find.sydney.edu.au, or research.monash.edu. The scholarship follows the supervisor, not the other way around.

For coursework merit scholarships

  • Each university's international scholarships page — most universities have a dedicated page listing all merit awards available to international students. Check these directly, not through aggregator sites.
  • Apply early — many coursework merit scholarships have rolling rounds or close when funds are exhausted. Applying in the first round of each year gives you the best chance.

A note on scholarship aggregator websites

Sites like ScholarshipPortal, Scholarships.com, and similar aggregators often list Australian scholarships with outdated deadlines, incorrect values, or descriptions for scholarships that no longer exist. Always verify every scholarship on the official university or government website before investing time in an application.

The step-by-step process

1

Identify your degree goal and study level

Research (PhD/Masters by Research) versus coursework (Masters/Bachelor's) opens up completely different scholarship pathways. Research students should pursue university funding packages; coursework students should target merit awards and Australia Awards.

2

Check your country's eligibility for Australia Awards

If your home country is on the DFAT participating countries list and you have 2+ years of post-bachelor's work experience, Australia Awards should be your first application. It is the highest-value scholarship you can realistically pursue and should not be skipped.

3

Research your field at Australian universities

Use QS Subject Rankings to identify the strongest Australian universities in your specific discipline. Rankings differ significantly by field — Monash leads in pharmacy, Melbourne in law and education, UNSW in engineering. Choose your target institution based on field strength and supervisor availability, not just overall ranking.

4

Contact potential supervisors (for research degrees)

Email 5–8 academics at your target university with a concise, targeted message — introduce yourself, reference a specific paper of theirs, and attach your CV and a one-page research outline. Do this 3–6 months before the scholarship deadline. Supervisor support is the deciding factor for research scholarship applications.

5

Prepare your IELTS or PTE score

Australia Awards requires IELTS Academic 6.5 (no band below 6.0). Research scholarship programs at Go8 universities require 6.5–7.0. Book your test early — centres in Lahore, Dhaka, Lagos, and Abuja fill quickly in January and February. Allow time for a resit.

6

Apply to 3–5 scholarships simultaneously

There is no rule against applying to multiple scholarships at the same time. Australia Awards + MGRS + USYDIS can all be active applications simultaneously. Much of the preparation overlaps — transcripts, CV, personal statement, referee reports. If you receive multiple offers, you choose which to accept.

7

Use each rejection to improve your next application

Rejection is the norm, not the exception. Australia Awards has a ~5–10% acceptance rate globally. Many successful scholarship holders applied 2–3 times. Contact the scholarship administrator for feedback after each unsuccessful application and use it specifically to improve your DILP, personal statement, or research proposal before the next cycle.

Common mistakes that cost applicants their scholarship

  • Applying for Australia Awards without enough work experience. You need at least 2 years of post-bachelor's professional experience. Applications from recent graduates are rarely competitive, regardless of academic results.
  • Writing a vague DILP. "I want to improve healthcare in my country" is not a DILP — it is a statement of ambition. Assessors need named organisations, specific roles, realistic timelines, and measurable impact. This is the most common reason strong candidates are rejected.
  • Contacting supervisors too late. Most international students email potential supervisors in the final 4 weeks before the deadline. The supervisors who are worth working with have already committed to candidates who contacted them months earlier. Start in January for an October deadline.
  • Applying to only one scholarship. There is no penalty for applying to multiple programs simultaneously. Students who apply to one scholarship and wait for the result before trying the next lose a full year each time they are unsuccessful.
  • Choosing a university by ranking alone. Studying at a #5-ranked university in your field is almost always better than studying at a #1-ranked university with a supervisor who is a poor match or unavailable. Supervisor quality and field-specific strength matter more than aggregate ranking for research outcomes.
  • Ignoring regional universities. The Destination Australia program offers AUD 15,000/year at regional universities with significantly less competition than metropolitan programs. Regional study also gives you an extra 1–2 years on your post-study work visa — a meaningful advantage for long-term career building in Australia.
