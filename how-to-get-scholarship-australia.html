Application strategy guide · 2026

How to Get a Scholarship in Australia as an International Student

A step-by-step guide to identifying the right scholarship, meeting eligibility requirements, finding a research supervisor, writing your application, and submitting across multiple awards simultaneously.

Step 1 — Understand which scholarships you actually qualify for

The single biggest mistake international students make is applying for scholarships they are not eligible for. Before writing a single word, spend 30 minutes mapping your eligibility against the four main funding streams in Australia:

🏛

Government scholarships (DFAT/Dept. of Education)

Australia Awards and the Research Training Program (RTP). Australia Awards is for citizens of ~50 developing partner countries. RTP is open to all nationalities but requires a confirmed research supervisor. These are the most generous — full funding including stipend.

🏫

University research scholarships

Melbourne MGRS, Sydney USYDIS, Monash MGS+MITS. Open to all nationalities for PhD and Masters by Research. Full tuition + AUD 37,000–42,754/year stipend. Requires a First Class degree and a confirmed supervisor.

🏆

University merit scholarships (coursework)

UNSW Scientia, ANU Chancellor’s, Monash International Merit. Partial tuition reductions (25–50% or AUD 10,000/yr) automatically assessed at admission. No stipend. Open to all nationalities with a Distinction average.

🌎

Regional and industry scholarships

Destination Australia (AUD 15,000/yr at regional campuses), Endeavour Awards (discontinued — replaced by Australia Awards), and industry-specific programs. Smaller pools but less competitive.

Step 3 — Meet the English language requirement early

Every Australian scholarship and visa requires an English language test result. Book early — test slots fill 6–8 weeks ahead, results take 2–3 weeks, and most scholarship portals require a valid score at submission.

  • IELTS Academic: 6.5 overall (no band below 6.0) for most scholarships and the Student Visa. Some faculties require 7.0. Most widely accepted.
  • PTE Academic: Equivalent to IELTS 6.5 is a PTE score of 58–64. Accepted by all major Australian universities and scholarship programs.
  • TOEFL iBT: 79+ overall. Accepted at most universities but some scholarship portals (notably OASIS for Australia Awards) require IELTS or PTE only.
  • English exemption: Citizens of Fiji, PNG and other English-medium education countries may be exempt — confirm with the scholarship provider before booking a test.

Step 4 — Find a research supervisor (for research scholarships)

This is the step most applicants skip — and the reason most research scholarship applications fail. The Melbourne MGRS, Sydney USYDIS, Monash MGS, and RTP all require a confirmed supervisor before you apply. Without one, your application is not competitive.

1

Identify 8–12 potential supervisors

Search each university’s research find-a-supervisor tool. Look for academics who publish in your exact research area. Read 2–3 of their recent papers before contacting them.

2

Write a targeted cold email (not a template)

Personalise each email: reference a specific paper they wrote, explain your research background, and propose a specific research question that fits their group. 5–6 sentences maximum. Attach your CV and a 1-page research proposal.

3

Follow up once after 2 weeks

A single polite follow-up is acceptable. More than one follow-up is not. If there is no response after follow-up, move to the next supervisor on your list.

4

Get a letter of support

Once a supervisor agrees to work with you, ask for a letter of support on university letterhead for your scholarship application. This letter significantly strengthens your file.

Contact supervisors 4–6 months before the scholarship deadline. Those worth working with fill their places early.

Step 5 — Write a compelling application

For Australia Awards, the critical document is the Development Impact and Linkages Plan (DILP). For research scholarships, it is your research proposal and personal statement. For coursework scholarships, the application is automatic — your transcripts speak for you.

  • DILP (Australia Awards): Must show a specific, implementable plan for how your study will translate into development impact in your home country. Generic plans score poorly. Assessors look for named organisations, measurable outcomes, and realistic timelines. Allow 3–4 weeks to draft. Our DILP overview covers the rubric in detail.
  • Research proposal: 1,000–2,000 words. Must define a specific, tractable research question; position it within existing literature; explain your methodology; and demonstrate why your supervisor’s group is the right fit. Write it with your supervisor’s input.
  • Personal statement / Statement of Purpose: Not a biography. Focus on your research trajectory, specific motivations, and what you will contribute to the program — not what you hope to get from it.

Step 6 — Apply to multiple scholarships simultaneously

There is no rule against applying for Australia Awards, the Melbourne MGRS, the Monash MGS, and the RTP all at the same time. The preparation overlaps significantly:

  • Certified transcripts — required by all
  • IELTS score — required by all
  • CV — required by all (tailor emphasis per application)
  • Referees — most need 2–3 references (ask the same referees, give them all forms at once)
  • Police clearance — required for Australia Awards
  • Research proposal — required for research scholarships, not Australia Awards
  • DILP — required for Australia Awards only

The additional time to apply to 4 scholarships versus 1 is typically 20–30% more work — and the payoff for a successful application is years of funded study.
