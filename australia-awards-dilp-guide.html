How to Write a Winning Development Impact and Linkages Plan (DILP) for Australia Awards 2026

The DILP is the single most decisive document in your Australia Awards application. It is scored, it is read carefully, and it separates applicants who win from applicants who don't. This guide walks you through every section with real examples from a successful scholar's winning DILP.

📋 Based on a real winning DILP — anonymised for your guidance

What is the DILP and why does it matter?

The Development Impact and Linkages Plan (DILP) is a mandatory document for Australia Awards applicants from participating countries including Pakistan, Bangladesh, Indonesia, Vietnam, and others. It must be submitted through the OASIS portal as part of your application, on or before the application deadline (30 April 14:00 AEST for most countries).

Unlike your personal statement — which talks about who you are — the DILP talks about what you will do when you go back. It forces you to think beyond the qualification itself and articulate a specific, credible plan for development impact in your home country.

This document is scored — not just submitted

The DILP is not a formality. Selection panels specifically assess how well your proposed study links to your long-term professional objectives and how convincingly you will address a development need in your country. A weak DILP from an otherwise strong candidate will cost them the scholarship. A strong DILP from a moderately competitive academic candidate can win it.

The DILP structure — what you need to complete

The DILP has two parts. Here is what each section asks you to do and what the selectors are looking for in each:

Part 1

Preparing for an Australia Awards Scholarship

This section helps you define your professional and personal goals as they relate to the development of your country. It also pushes you to think about activities beyond your formal degree that can make your experience more relevant and meaningful.

Priority Sectors (tick up to two)

You select up to two priority sectors from the following list. Your choices must align with your proposed study and your professional background:

Water Security
Infrastructure
Climate Change
Health
Education
Agriculture
Empowerment of Women & Girls
Inclusive Economic Development

Strategic tip on sector selection

Only tick sectors where you have direct professional experience and where your proposed course clearly applies. Ticking "Climate Change" because it sounds important — when you're a civil engineer working on local water infrastructure — is a red flag. Selectors are looking for coherence between: your job, your sectors, your course, and your DILP plan.

Planned contribution to your country's development (250–500 words)

Word limit: 250–500 words

This is where most applicants write generic, forgettable content. The winning approach is radically different: be hyper-specific about a real, named problem you are currently dealing with in your job — not a general national issue.

❌ Weak example — generic and forgettableLikely rejected

"Pakistan faces significant challenges in water supply and sanitation. After my scholarship, I plan to use my knowledge to improve water and sanitation systems in Pakistan and help address the water crisis facing the country."

✅ Strong example — specific, credible, evidence-basedWinning approach

"I am currently working as a technical officer in a municipal development authority responsible for infrastructure and public services in a small township of approximately 7,200 residents. The township faces two urgent, interconnected public health problems that I am directly responsible for addressing in my role.

First, the existing water supply system draws from an infiltration gallery that is contaminated by untreated waste from nearby tributaries — a problem the original designers failed to account for. Residents are currently drinking contaminated water, contributing to rising rates of waterborne disease. Second, the sewerage network has no functioning disposal mechanism, meaning untreated sewage is entering the groundwater system and surrounding environment.

After completing my Masters in Environmental Engineering, I plan to design and implement two specific projects: (a) a water supply system upgrade to remove contamination from the infiltration gallery, and (b) a sustainable sewage disposal system. Both projects are already listed as priorities in the Annual Development Plan (ADP) of our department — but could not be designed or implemented due to insufficient technical expertise. My study will provide exactly the technical knowledge required."

Notice the difference. The strong example: names a specific community, quantifies the problem (population, number of houses), identifies the exact technical gap, and explains precisely how the study fills it. The selector can picture the applicant returning to their job and implementing a real project.

Part 1B

Technical skills, competencies and learning opportunities

Five technical skills to enhance (no word limit — list format)

List five specific technical skills you need to acquire during your scholarship. The key word is technical — not soft skills, not generic competencies. Be precise.

✅ Strong technical skills listWinning approach

1. Design of sustainable water supply systems
2. Design of wastewater treatment facilities
3. Wastewater treatment processes in municipal corporations
4. Conversion of waste to energy — techniques and processes
5. Effective local government / city council operations in developed cities

All five skills directly connect to the specific problem described in the contribution section. They are concrete enough that a selection panel can assess whether the proposed course actually teaches them.

Competencies to strengthen

Tick the relevant competencies from the provided list (Career & Self-development, Communication, Critical Thinking, Equity & Inclusion, Leadership, Professionalism, Teamwork, Technology). You must then write a brief elaboration on the ones you have selected.

Tip on competency elaboration

Don't tick all competencies — it looks unfocused. Select 2–3 that genuinely apply to your gap and write specific, honest explanations. For example: if you tick "Technology," explain that your current public sector organisation still relies on manual processes, and that you specifically plan to learn technologies used by comparable organisations in Australia (like the municipal water authority) to bring those practices back.

Additional learning opportunities in Australia

This section asks you to identify learning opportunities beyond your formal degree — internships, professional body memberships, site visits, workshops, and networking. This is critically important and many applicants leave it vague.

✅ Strong learning opportunities — specific and credibleWinning approach

"I will seek a guided tour or internship at a major municipal water treatment plant in Australia to observe best practices in water treatment, operations and management. Part-time work or an internship at a relevant utility organisation will help me understand treatment processes, technologies, and management systems used in Australian municipal agencies. I will use my university faculty connections to pursue this opportunity. I will also seek membership with Engineers Australia to build professional networks and access ongoing technical guidance after returning home."

Australian experts and organisations to partner with

Identify specific Australian organisations you plan to engage during your scholarship and describe the nature of that partnership. This demonstrates you have done your research — not just applied to "study in Australia" generally.

✅ Strong partnerships tableWinning approach

Organisation 1: A major city water utility
Nature of partnership: Internship or part-time work at a water treatment facility to observe treatment capacity, operations, and best practices in water quality management.

Organisation 2: City Local Government / City Council
Nature of partnership: Volunteering or internship to observe how an advanced municipality delivers public services to residents on a daily basis — applicable to improving service delivery in my home municipality.

Organisation 3: Community/professional group related to your field
Nature of partnership: Engaging with peers from multiple countries to discuss how municipalities manage waste — building international professional networks for ongoing knowledge exchange.

Part 2

Planning to be a leader for development

This is the most important and most heavily scored section. It asks you to outline a specific, practical, doable plan for contributing to development in your country using your Australia Awards experience — technical skills, competencies, networks, and linkages.

Title of your proposed activity

Write a clear, specific title. Not "Improving water in Pakistan" — but something like: "Upgrade and improvement of water supply and sewerage network in [specific type of area]." The title should immediately communicate what you are going to do and where.

Implementation period and duration

Be realistic. A credible plan has two phases: a design/planning phase (3–4 months) and an implementation/execution phase (up to 24 months). Many applicants write plans that are either too short (unrealistic) or too vague ("when I return to work").

Rationale and Intended Outcomes (500 words)

Word limit: 500 words

This section has four required elements. A strong response addresses all four explicitly:

  1. The specific problem — name the problem, quantify it, explain who is affected
  2. Why it matters — connect it to public health, national development priorities, or economic impact
  3. How your Australia Awards experience helps — specifically link your course and the linkages (internships, partnerships) to the solution
  4. What you will achieve — measurable, concrete outcomes
✅ Strong rationale — all four elements presentWinning approach

Problem: "There are two critical issues currently being faced by residents of the target township. The existing water supply system is contaminated due to untreated waste from local tributaries — a significant health risk. Additionally, untreated sewage is being disposed into the environment, contaminating groundwater and potentially spreading waterborne diseases."

Importance: "Both issues directly impact the health and wellbeing of approximately 7,200 residents. Clean water and safe sewage disposal are foundational to public health and environmental sustainability. The proper design and implementation of water and wastewater treatment systems will improve quality of life and protect the local environment."

How Australia Awards helps: "My Masters program will provide the technical knowledge and practical exposure needed to design sustainable water and waste treatment systems. I will learn from experts in the field and gain firsthand experience through site visits, internships and part-time work at a major water corporation. Observing advanced municipal operations will help me understand best practices in local government and public service provision."

Achievements / Changes: "By addressing water contamination, residents will have access to clean drinking water, reducing health risks. Groundwater will be protected after sewage is properly treated. Sustainable water and sewage management practices will contribute to long-term health of the township and local environment."

Activity and Tasks (500 words)

Word limit: 500 words

List specific, practical, doable activities you will undertake after returning home. Every item must be something within your own capacity and authority to implement. Do not write activities that depend entirely on other people's decisions or large government approvals you have no control over.

The "within your own capacity" test

Before writing any activity, ask: "Can I actually do this on my own initiative, in my current role, without requiring someone else's approval?" Activities like "design a system" or "prepare a proposal" pass this test. Activities like "the government will implement a national policy" fail it. Your plan must be personal and achievable.

✅ Strong activities list — specific and within own capacityWinning approach

After returning, I plan to implement the following activities within my capacity as an experienced technical professional in urban development and public service:

1. Design a water supply system to remove contamination from the water source
2. Prepare and submit a project proposal for approval through the official funding mechanism
3. Supervise the execution of the water supply system and monitor its effectiveness through regular water quality tests
4. Design a safe and effective sewage disposal system for the target area
5. Prepare and submit a second project proposal for sewage system funding
6. Supervise sewage system implementation and monitor groundwater impact
7. Train other technical officers in my department on water and sewage management best practices
8. Organise community awareness events with the local residents' association to promote clean water habits and safe waste disposal
9. Collaborate with Australian counterparts (established during my scholarship) for ongoing technical guidance after completion
10. Collect resident feedback through surveys to measure the impact of the projects

Measure of Success

How will you know if your plan has worked? What evidence will you have? This section must be specific and measurable — not "residents will be happy."

✅ Strong measures of success — concrete and measurableWinning approach

My proposal will be successful if the following results are achieved:
✓ Consistent improvement in water quality test results over time — providing scientific evidence of success
✓ Positive feedback and increased satisfaction levels from residents, measured through the local residents' welfare association
✓ Decrease in reported cases of waterborne diseases such as cholera — providing concrete evidence of improved public health

Output — tangible accomplishments

What are the physical, concrete outputs of your plan? This is different from outcomes. Outcomes are the impact (healthier residents). Outputs are the things you will build, write, or create (a water treatment plant, a training manual, a policy proposal).

Direct beneficiaries

Who directly benefits from your activities? Be specific — name the community, the approximate number of people, and other organisations that will benefit from the knowledge transfer.

Partners

Identify potential partners for your proposed activity. Include government agencies, community organisations, and professional bodies. For each, describe the specific nature of the collaboration.

Resources Required

Specify what financial, material, or institutional support you need to implement your plan — and where it will come from. Be realistic. If your plan requires government budget approval, explain the process (e.g., Annual Development Plan submission). Do not assume Australia Awards will fund implementation.

Challenges and Risks — with mitigating actions

Identify up to three major challenges. For each, describe what you will do to minimise or overcome it. Common challenges in development contexts include funding gaps, community resistance, political changes, extreme weather events, and O&M after completion. Show that you have thought through these realistically.

✅ Strong challenges table — honest and specificWinning approach

Challenge 1: Inadequate funding
Mitigation: The implementing authority already has self-financing capacity. Additional funding can be secured by increasing rates for municipal services provided to residents. Project will also be submitted for inclusion in the Annual Development Plan to access government funding.

Challenge 2: O&M after completion
Mitigation: Training of O&M staff before and after project completion. Collaboration with Australian counterparts established during the scholarship for ongoing technical guidance. Climate-resilient design principles incorporated from the start.

Challenge 3: Community resistance (e.g. land acquisition)
Mitigation: Proactive community engagement from the planning stage. Informing residents of the positive health impacts of the projects and the negative consequences of non-implementation. Building local ownership through residents' welfare associations.

Adapting the DILP for different professional fields

The DILP structure is the same regardless of your field. What changes is the specific problem, the technical skills, and the Australian organisations you identify. Here is how applicants from different fields should approach it:

Health and Public Health

Identify a specific health system gap in your work context — a hospital with no functioning disease surveillance system, a region with high maternal mortality rates and no obstetric capacity, a health facility with broken cold chain infrastructure. Your Australian linkages should include a hospital, a public health agency, or a university research centre focused on health systems. Measurable outcomes: reduction in disease incidence rates, improvement in health facility utilisation data, number of health workers trained.

Education

Focus on a specific, observable gap in your school, district, or curriculum — not "Pakistan's education system is poor." For example: teacher professional development is absent in your district, digital learning infrastructure doesn't exist in a specific number of schools, or girls' dropout rates in your area are above the national average. Australian linkages: a university education faculty, a school system, or a teacher training organisation. Measurable outcomes: number of teachers trained, student retention rates, literacy test improvements.

Engineering and Infrastructure

This is the strongest field for DILP writing because problems are concrete and measurable. Identify a specific structure, network, or system in poor condition or missing entirely. The Australian linkage is typically a relevant engineering firm, utility company, or local government authority. Measurable outcomes: infrastructure quality tests, number of beneficiaries, reduction in incidents/failures.

Agriculture and Food Security

Identify a specific crop, region, farming community, or supply chain gap. Avoid "food security in Pakistan" — focus on "smallholder farmer productivity in [type of region] due to [specific practice gap]." Australian linkages: agricultural research institutes, university agronomy departments, or farming cooperatives. Measurable outcomes: yield improvement %, adoption rates of new practices, household income improvement.

Governance and Public Policy

This is the hardest field to write a specific DILP for, because outputs are less tangible. Focus on a specific policy gap, a failed process, or a missing regulatory framework in your direct area of work. Australian linkages: government departments, think tanks, or public administration schools. Be very specific about what you will produce (a policy paper, a reformed procedure, a new training module) and how you will measure adoption.

The most common DILP mistakes — and how to avoid them

  • Too vague: "I will contribute to Pakistan's development in the water sector" — this tells the panel nothing about your plan or your credibility
  • No link to your job: Writing a DILP about a sector you don't work in, or a plan you couldn't implement in your actual role
  • Activities beyond your authority: Writing "the government will build a new national water treatment system" — you cannot control this
  • Unmeasurable outcomes: "Residents will have better health" — how will you measure this? When? What is the baseline?
  • Generic Australian linkages: "I will visit Australian universities" — name a specific institution and describe a specific type of engagement
  • Ignoring the climate change prompt: The DILP template explicitly asks how climate change might affect your plan. Many applicants skip this — it costs points
  • Over-ambitious timeline: A plan to transform an entire city's infrastructure in 6 months after returning is not credible
  • Exceeding word limits: Selectors notice when applicants ignore the 500-word limit. It signals poor attention to detail

Top 5 DILP writing tips from successful scholars

  1. Start with the problem, not the solution. The most compelling DILPs open with a vivid, specific description of a real problem the applicant sees every day at work — not a solution they want to implement. The problem makes the selector care. The solution comes after.
  2. Use numbers wherever possible. Numbers anchor your credibility: "7,200 residents", "900 houses", "a treatment capacity of X litres per day", "400 workers in my department". Vague claims are forgettable. Specific numbers are memorable.
  3. Name specific Australian organisations and explain exactly why them. "I will visit Melbourne Water Corporation's Werribee Treatment Plant because it has one of the largest treatment capacities in the country and directly relevant to the scale of system I will design" is far stronger than "I will visit Australian water companies."
  4. Connect every element. Your sectors → your job → your course → your activities → your outcomes → your partners should form a single coherent chain. If any link is missing, the plan falls apart. Read your DILP as if you are a sceptical selector: does everything connect?
  5. Write your challenges honestly. The challenges section is where many applicants get defensive and write "there are no major challenges." Selectors don't believe this. Writing genuine challenges (funding gaps, political changes, community resistance) and credible mitigating actions shows maturity and realism — and wins points.
