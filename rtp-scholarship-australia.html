Research Training Program (RTP) Scholarship Australia 2026

The Research Training Program is Australia's largest government-funded research scholarship — awarded at every Australian university, open to all nationalities, covering full tuition fees plus a tax-free living stipend. If you are applying for a PhD or Masters by Research in Australia, this is the scholarship you need to understand.

What is the Research Training Program (RTP)?

The Research Training Program (RTP) is a block grant program funded by the Australian Government through the Department of Education. The government distributes funding annually to all 43 eligible Australian universities, which then award individual RTP scholarships to their highest-ranked PhD and Masters by Research students.

The RTP replaced the International Postgraduate Research Scholarship (IPRS) in January 2017. Unlike its predecessor, the RTP has no separate national application — you apply through your chosen university, and the scholarship is awarded as part of the admission process. This makes it fundamentally different from Australia Awards or other competitive scholarships: the university does the scholarship selection, not the government.

The RTP is Australia's most broadly awarded research scholarship. Thousands of international students hold RTP scholarships at any given time, across every field of study and every major Australian city.

Key fact — RTP is not one scholarship, it's a framework

When universities advertise "full scholarship packages" for PhD students, they are almost always referring to an RTP Fee Offset (covers tuition) combined with an RTP Stipend (covers living costs). Some universities brand these together as their own named scholarship (e.g. Melbourne Graduate Research Scholarship, Sydney USYDIS) — but the funding ultimately comes from the federal RTP block grant.

What does the RTP scholarship cover?

A full RTP package typically includes three components:

1. RTP Fees Offset

Covers 100% of your tuition fees for the minimum duration of your program — up to 4 years for a PhD, up to 2 years for a Masters by Research. International student tuition fees at Australian universities range from AUD $28,000–$50,000 per year, so the fee offset is worth AUD $112,000–$200,000 over a standard PhD.

2. RTP Stipend

A tax-free living allowance paid fortnightly. The 2026 rates vary by university — from approximately AUD $34,315 (base government rate) to AUD $42,754 at the University of Sydney. The stipend is generally tax-exempt for the first 4 years of a PhD under Australian tax law.

3. RTP Allowances (varies by university)

May include: Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC) for international students, relocation allowance (AUD $1,500–$3,000 for students moving from overseas), thesis allowance, and conference travel support. Not all universities provide all allowances — check the specific scholarship conditions at your target institution.

What the RTP does NOT cover

The RTP does not cover return airfare to/from Australia, pre-arrival costs, English language test fees, or living costs above the stipend. Unlike Australia Awards, there is no establishment allowance or introductory academic program. Budget separately for these costs.

Why the stipend difference matters

There is a AUD $8,439/year difference between the highest (Sydney, $42,754) and lowest (ACU, $34,315) RTP stipends. Over a 3.5-year PhD, that's a $29,500 difference in take-home income. However, cost of living also varies significantly — Sydney is considerably more expensive than Adelaide or Canberra. Factor in rent, transport, and groceries when comparing universities, not just the stipend headline figure.

Eligibility requirements

The RTP is open to a very broad group of students. To be eligible you must:

  • Be admitted (or applying for admission) to a PhD or Masters by Research program at an eligible Australian university
  • Be enrolled full-time (part-time is possible in limited circumstances — disability, carer responsibilities)
  • Not have previously completed a degree at the same level at an Australian institution
  • Meet the English language requirements of your university and program
  • Have a confirmed or prospective supervisor (strongly recommended before applying)

There is no country restriction — unlike Australia Awards, the RTP is available to citizens of every country including India, China, the USA, and European nations. There is also no minimum work experience requirement — academic merit and research potential are the sole criteria.

Academic merit requirements

Competition for RTP scholarships is fierce. Selection is based primarily on:

  • Academic results — typically a First Class Honours degree or equivalent (WAM 75%+, GPA 3.5/4.0 or higher for most Group of Eight universities)
  • Research publications — peer-reviewed publications significantly strengthen your ranking, especially for competitive universities
  • Research proposal — a clear, feasible, well-scoped proposal aligned with your supervisor's research
  • Referee reports — typically 2 academic referees who can speak to your research potential
  • Supervisor support — a confirmed supervisor who has space and funding is often a prerequisite, not just helpful

The supervisor rule — the most important thing nobody tells you

At most Australian Group of Eight universities, your application will not be ranked competitively without a confirmed supervisor. Supervisors have limited capacity (typically 4–6 research students at a time) and need to formally support your application. Email potential supervisors 3–6 months before the scholarship deadline. Introduce yourself with your CV, transcripts, and a 1-page research proposal. One positive supervisor response changes everything.

How to apply — step by step

Unlike most scholarships, the RTP has no central application portal. You apply through your chosen university. Here is how the process typically works:

01

Identify your target university and supervisor

Search academic staff profiles at your target university. Find 3–5 potential supervisors whose research aligns with yours. Email them with your CV and a concise research proposal. Wait for a positive response before proceeding.

02

Check the scholarship round deadlines

Each university runs 2–3 scholarship rounds per year with different deadlines. International students typically need to apply 2–3 months before domestic students. Missing the round deadline means waiting 6 months for the next round.

03

Apply for admission

Submit your admission application through the university's graduate research portal. Include your research proposal, transcripts, English test results (IELTS 6.5+ typically), CV, and referee contact details. In most cases, ticking the "scholarship" box in the admission form is all that's needed to be considered for the RTP.

04

Referees submit their reports

The system automatically sends requests to your nominated referees once you submit your application. Referees must submit before the scholarship deadline — your application is not considered complete until both reports are received. Alert referees well in advance.

05

Wait for a scholarship offer

Universities rank eligible applicants and release scholarship offers — typically 4–8 weeks after the deadline. Offers are usually conditional on confirming enrolment. You have a limited window (usually 4–6 weeks) to accept the offer.

The biggest practical difference: RTP holders can apply for the Temporary Graduate visa (Subclass 485) after completing their degree and stay in Australia to work, while Australia Awards scholars must leave for 2 years first. If staying in Australia after graduation is a goal, RTP is the better pathway.

Tips for a competitive RTP application

1. Contact supervisors before anything else

This is repeated everywhere — because it is the single most important step. A supervisor who actively supports your application will advocate for you in the scholarship ranking process. A generic application without supervisor support is almost always ranked lower.

2. Aim for publications if possible

At competitive universities (Melbourne, Sydney, ANU, UNSW), applicants with peer-reviewed publications — even just one — score significantly higher. If you are still in your final year of undergraduate or Honours, consider submitting a paper based on your thesis research before applying.

3. Apply to multiple universities simultaneously

There is no restriction on applying to multiple Australian universities at the same time. Apply to your top 3 choices. The scholarship and admission offers are not binding until you accept — you can compare offers and choose the best one.

4. Choose your round deadline carefully

The October/November rounds at most universities are for a February start. The March/April rounds are for a July start. If you are not ready for one round, wait for the next — do not rush a weak application.

5. Write a sharp research proposal

Your research proposal does not need to be long (1,500–3,000 words is typical), but it must demonstrate: a clearly defined research question, familiarity with the existing literature, a feasible methodology, and a realistic timeline. Supervisors and scholarship committees both read the proposal — it is your single most important document.
